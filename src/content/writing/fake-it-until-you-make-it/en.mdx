Today I had the chance to hear from accomplished engineers, including Ryan Lahlou from J.P. Morgan and Ricardo Anaya from Qualcomm. They shared advice on how
to grow, adapt, and thrive as an engineer. The person who stood out to me most was Y.C. Wang.

Y.C. spoke about his journey as a hardware engineer in Silicon Valley, and how the mindset of "fake it until you make it" helped him get to where he is today.
What stayed with me was not the idea of pretending to know everything, but the opposite: being willing to step into unfamiliar situations, learn quickly, and
grow through action.

He emphasized that engineers, now more than ever, need to become lifelong learners. **I could not agree more.**

AI, the internet, and the printing press all changed the way people learn. Each of these technologies expanded access to knowledge and raised the expectations
for what people could do with it. As an aspiring engineer, I see it as my responsibility to keep learning, building, and improving.

Ryan also mentioned the value of building proof of concepts and finding ways to create value, sometimes before you fully realize the impact of what you are
doing. The more I hear from experienced engineers, the more I realize how fortunate I am to be learning and building in this moment.

## What I Took From This Talk

Learning is a lifelong journey, but learning without action is incomplete. The talk reminded me that I should:

- Build practical things instead of only consuming information.
- Stay modern and avoid getting stuck in outdated ways of thinking.
- Put myself in situations where I have to learn quickly.
- Stay agentic when I feel uncertain, blocked, or behind.
- Treat gaps in my knowledge as invitations to improve, not reasons to stop.

## What I Am Still Thinking About

When I compare what I know today with what I knew a year ago, the difference is clear. I want that pattern to continue. I want to keep looking back and seeing
that I became more capable because I kept learning and applying what I learned.

Maybe AI will not replace all of us. Maybe it will mostly empower the people with enough curiosity, discipline, and hunger to keep learning.

Being honest about what you do not know is essential. But being committed to learning it, applying it, and improving is what makes someone grow as an engineer.

Learning is the means; implementing and building are the proof.
