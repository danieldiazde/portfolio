I am a Data Science and Math student at Tec, and I have spent the last few semesters trying to figure out what "becoming an AI engineer" actually means. This post is a snapshot of where my thinking has landed so far — it will change.

## Three things I optimize for

1. **Fundamentals over frameworks.** The frameworks change every year. The linear algebra, probability, and systems intuition behind them do not.
2. **Building from scratch.** A reimplementation that fits in one head teaches more than a tutorial that fits in one repo. That is why Irongrad exists.
3. **Engineering taste, not just demos.** Anyone can ship a demo with a modern model behind it. What separates good systems from impressive screenshots is what happens at the edges — error handling, evaluation, data drift, cost.

## What I am still figuring out

- How to balance breadth (touching ML, systems, product) with the depth a research lab expects.
- When to stop reading and start writing code.
- How much of "AI engineer" is going to mean "the person who knows where the model is wrong" five years from now.

This post will keep getting rewritten.
