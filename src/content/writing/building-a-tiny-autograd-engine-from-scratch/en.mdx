This is a working draft of the notes that grew out of building **Irongrad**, a homemade autograd and deep learning framework. The goal of the project is not to compete with PyTorch — it is to internalize the design decisions that make a tensor library feel natural to use.

## Why build one

It is easy to learn deep learning through high-level frameworks and never touch the substrate underneath. Building a small autograd engine forces you to confront the shape of the abstraction:

- A tensor is a contiguous buffer plus a shape and a stride.
- An operation is a function plus a recipe for its gradient.
- A computation graph is the trace of those operations, kept alive long enough to be differentiated.

Once those three pieces fit together, almost every framework decision — eager vs. graph mode, autodiff modes, custom kernels — starts to feel principled instead of magical.

## What is in scope

The framework supports a small but growing surface area:

- Element-wise operations and broadcasting
- Reductions (`sum`, `mean`, `max`)
- Matrix multiplication
- Reverse-mode autodiff via a topological sort over the graph
- A thin Python binding over C++20 kernels through pybind11

Notebooks will follow as the kernels stabilize.

## What I am learning

Two things keep surprising me:

1. **Most of the work is bookkeeping.** Storing references, freeing buffers, getting strides right — the actual math is a small fraction of the code.
2. **The boundary between Python and C++ is where most of the performance lives.** Crossing it once per op is cheap; crossing it once per element is fatal.
